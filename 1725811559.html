Let's solve the integral of \(2x + 3\) from 1 to 4. The integral we need to solve is:

\( \int_{1}^{4} (2x + 3) \, dx \)

We will handle this step-by-step:

1. First, we find the antiderivative (indefinite integral) of \(2x + 3\).

To do this, we integrate each term separately:

\( \int 2x \, dx \) and \( \int 3 \, dx \)

2. The antiderivative of \(2x\) is:

\( \int 2x \, dx = x^2 \)

3. The antiderivative of \(3\) is:

\( \int 3 \, dx = 3x \)

4. Combining these, the antiderivative of \(2x + 3\) is:

\( x^2 + 3x \)

5. Now we apply the limits of integration from 1 to 4. We use the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, which states:

\( \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \)

Here, \(F(x)\) is the antiderivative of \(f(x)\).

So, we need to evaluate \(F(x) = x^2 + 3x\) at the upper limit (4) and the lower limit (1) and subtract:

\( F(4) = 4^2 + 3(4) = 16 + 12 = 28 \)

\( F(1) = 1^2 + 3(1) = 1 + 3 = 4 \)</
